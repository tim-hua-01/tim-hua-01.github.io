More of my work history

    In a past life, I was an economist on Walmart's Economics Team working with pricing and Spark drivers. I designed and executed complex (multi-treatment arms with possible spillover effects) experiments and applied causal machine learning methods such as DoubleML and generalized random forests. I also used synthetic difference in differences and Chernozhukov et al. (2021) standard errors for evaluating treatment effects for when only one unit was treated. During my internship in 2022, I came up with this graph-partitioning based method for grouping stores into clusters for a pricing experiment. As of August 2024, AIs are almost able to independently come up with the method when prompted.
    I left in August 2023 because I was just really interested in AI safety. Like I would read papers and listen to three hour long youtube videos about transformers on the subway. I would go to the Brookfield place food court after work and do ARENA exercises. At some point, I decided that I should at least try to make the thing I really enjoy the thing I work on all day. So I quit my job and moved to Berkeley without any specific opportunity lined up. I did some random interp projects got rejected by a bunch of things for four months, then got into MARS and AI safety camp, and now I'm at MATS. Right now there's a blob on the random page for some AI safety content I would recommend for anyone interested in the field.
    I am also still interested in economics, and will probably want to do some writing about the economics of transformative AI at some point.

    I graduated from Middlebury College with a degree in Economics in May 2023. My senior thesis, Fox News's Effect on Social and Moral Preferences, won the D.K. Smith Prize in Economics for best thesis (and got cited by Ben Enke). I spent my 2021-2022 academic year as a visiting undergraduate student at Harvard, where I worked as a research assistant for Professor Desmond Ang investigating racial stereotypes in films by scraping data from TV Tropes. I also took Econ 1011a, the college's advanced microeconomic theory class. To give future students a better idea of how economists use the methods we learned, I wrote starred sections in the student-written textbook. They explain several examples of models found in actual papers. Honestly I would love to teach an economic theory class where we exclusively look at econ models that have nothing to do with money, like how to implement an optimal rain prayer strategy to increase belief in religious authorities. I am still very interested in economics and recently took a two-week course in the economics of transformative AI.
     From June 2021 to May 2022, I worked on Professor Carol Graham's team. Our paper exploring the conflicting reports of mental health during the pandemic from different surveys and the connections between well-being and deaths of despair can be found here. During the 2020-2021 academic year, I worked as a Research Assistant to Professor Martin Abel on a paper studying how different payment schemes affect worker effort. I also completed a personal research project examining COVID-19 tweets by governors and health experts with Chris Kim, Zihan Zhang, and Professor Alex Lyford during my second semester at college (I took a gap semester, where I got started with my hobby of looking at old books). Our paper was published in the Journal of Student Research.
