Q4:

    The internet has allowed people with very peculiar or specialized interest from around to world to cluster together and pursue these interests through online communities. This is both very exciting and scary. While it feels like increased access to the internet has greatly amplified mainstream culture and made society more homogenous, the ability to connect with like-minded individuals from around the world has engendered specialized communities. In a sense, the internet has created the potential for greater heterogeneity.

    The scary part comes from communities such as QAnon and this other website that teachers individuals how to kill themselves. One effect of the internet is that, by breaking down geographical boundaries, it makes being crazy appear a lot more sane. Suppose that you are among the 0.1% in terms of craziness on some issue. Then it is unlikely that you would ever find someone with the same views as you on a daily basis without the internet, and that means that you are likely to give up on being so extreme. However, the internet means that some others in the 0.001% can set up a forum, where the crazies take up a majority. Now, you can log on reddit, and it appears that plenty of people are just like yourself, and you'll have a sense of community around the extreme belief. The internet sustains craziness.

     However, craziness isn't all bad, and that's what excites me. Personally, I am among the top 0.005% in terms of my fascination with beautiful watches. I find great joy in discussion and learning about watches, and the hobbie would have been much more difficult without the internet. I also follow fairly obscure subreddits such as r/polcompballs, where people draw comics with small balls representing the over 400 canon ideologies. All these divesre communities all have their own customs, language, and of course, folklore. I would love to take a class to study them!

The second piece of context is the definition of an ordinal number. Ordinal numbers are numbers you use to indicate order (e.g., first, fifth, tenth, etc.). They don't really have a size but only an order. Thus, we can have something called the limit ordinal, typically depicted with the greek letter omega, that represents the number that comes after all of the natural numbers. And then we could have ω + 1, ω + 2... and eventually, ω + ω, which gives us ω * 2, and then we could have ω * 3, ω *10, and eventually ω * ω or ω2, and so on. Ordinal numbers can be used to prove certain things in mathematics, and is a fairly advanced piece of math not typically covered in undergraduate curriculums.
